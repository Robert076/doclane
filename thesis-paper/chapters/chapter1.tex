\chapter{Introduction}
\label{intro}

\section{Introducing the problem}

\par Public institutions are among the most essential points of contact between the state and its citizens, handling everything from official documents and permits to a wide range of administrative procedures. Given how frequently people depend on these services, the efficiency and reliability of the underlying processes matter considerably, both for citizens trying to complete routine tasks and for the institutions themselves trying to manage high volumes of requests.

\par The reality in many public institutions, however, is that these processes still rely heavily on physical documents and in-person interactions. Citizens are regularly required to travel to an office, wait in queues, and hand over printed paperwork just to initiate what are often straightforward requests. This model creates predictable problems: long waiting times, repeated visits when something is missing, and a general inefficiency in how information flows between citizens and the institution. Physical documents can be lost or damaged in transit or storage, and errors introduced during manual processing can delay procedures that are sometimes time-sensitive. Taken together, these issues point to a clear need for rethinking how document-based workflows are handled in public administration.

\section{The role of information technology in public administration and limitations of existing digital solutions}

\par The adoption of digital systems in public institutions has grown steadily over the past decade, and for good reason. Software platforms allow institutions to manage large volumes of data with greater consistency, automate repetitive steps that would otherwise require manual effort, and offer citizens access to services without requiring a physical visit. Where these transitions have been implemented well, the results are visible: shorter processing times, fewer errors from manual handling, and greater transparency about where a request stands at any given moment.

\par That said, the picture is uneven. Many of the digital tools currently in use were built for specific services or individual institutions, which means a citizen dealing with multiple types of requests may still need to navigate several disconnected systems. Some platforms cover only part of the workflow, handling document submission but not validation, or validation but not feedback, leaving gaps that still require manual intervention or in-person follow-up. Financial constraints, insufficient technical infrastructure, and limited digital literacy among both staff and citizens have also slowed adoption in many places.

\par The result is a landscape where digital tools exist but rarely cover the full process end-to-end, and where the absence of a consistent, reusable workflow structure means each institution effectively solves the same problem from scratch. This is the gap the present work attempts to address.

\section{Proposed solution, objectives, and scope}

\par The system developed as part of this thesis, Doclane, is a web-based application designed to support document-based workflows between citizens and public institutions. Users can submit requests, upload the required documents, and follow the status of their application through the platform. On the institutional side, employees can review submissions, validate documents, and communicate feedback directly through the system, without needing to resort to separate channels or manual record-keeping.

\par The core objective is to reduce the dependency on physical visits and paper-based handling by embedding the entire request lifecycle into a structured digital workflow. This includes making the process more legible for citizens, who are shown exactly what documents are required at each step and receive timely feedback if something needs to be corrected or resubmitted. The broader technical goals guiding the implementation are: a backend capable of handling document requests reliably, secure authentication with role-based access, cloud storage for file management, and an architecture that can be extended or adapted without significant rework.

\par It is worth noting what the system does not do. Doclane is a support tool, not a replacement for institutional authority. Final decisions regarding document approval remain with authorised personnel within each institution, and the system does not alter or bypass any part of the legal process. Its role is to organise and digitise the workflow around those decisions, making the process faster and more transparent without changing who holds responsibility for the outcome.
